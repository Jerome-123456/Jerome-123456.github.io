Drugs

Why Do People Use Drugs?

  • Curiosity and Experimentation – Especially among young people, the desire to try something new.
  • Peer Pressure – Influence from friends or social groups.
  • Stress Relief – Coping with anxiety, depression, or difficult life situations.
  • Escapism – To escape from reality or emotional pain.
  • Recreation and Pleasure – Seeking euphoria or a temporary high.
  • Addiction – Continued use due to physical or psychological dependence.
  • Cultural or Social Norms – In some settings, drug use is normalized or even expected.
  • Medical Reasons – Using prescribed drugs that can lead to misuse.

What to Do If You're Offered Drugs

  • Say “No” Clearly – Be firm and direct. You don’t need to give a reason.
  • Change the Subject – Shift the conversation to something else.
  • Walk Away – It’s okay to remove yourself from the situation.
  • Use Humor – A joke can defuse the pressure.
  • Blame Something Else – Say you have responsibilities, like a sports game or family.
  • Have a Plan – Practice responses ahead of time.
  • Surround Yourself with Supportive Friends – Stay around people who respect your choices.
  • Know That It’s Okay to Say No – Real friends will understand and respect your decision.

What to Do If You’ve Used Drugs

  • Don’t Panic – Stay calm. Panicking can make the situation worse.
  • Get to a Safe Place – Find somewhere quiet and safe where you can rest if needed.
  • Tell Someone You Trust – Reach out to a friend, family member, teacher, or counselor.
  • Seek Medical Help if Needed – If you're feeling unwell, call emergency services or go to a hospital.
  • Avoid Taking More – Don't mix with other substances or continue using.
  • Reflect on the Experience – Think about why it happened and how you feel about it.
  • Talk to a Professional – A doctor, counselor, or therapist can help you process it and make a plan.
  • Make a Positive Change – Use the experience to grow and consider healthier coping strategies.

What to Do If You're Addicted to Drugs

  • Acknowledge the Problem – Admitting you need help is the first step.
  • Talk to Someone You Trust – Reach out to a friend, family member, teacher, or mentor.
  • Seek Professional Help – Contact a doctor, therapist, or addiction specialist.
  • Find a Support Group – Groups like Narcotics Anonymous (NA) offer community and guidance.
  • Avoid Triggers – Stay away from people, places, or situations that encourage drug use.
  • Create Healthy Habits – Focus on sleep, nutrition, and physical activity.
  • Be Patient With Yourself – Recovery is a journey; setbacks happen but don’t define you.
  • Build a New Routine – Fill your life with positive activities and supportive people.

Common Drugs and Their Effects

Drug Effects
Alcohol Slows brain activity, causes relaxation and lowered inhibitions; long-term use can damage the liver.
Cannabis (Marijuana) Causes relaxation, altered perception, memory issues, and slower reaction time.
Cocaine Increases energy and alertness; may cause euphoria, anxiety, and heart problems.
Ecstasy (MDMA) Boosts mood, empathy, and energy; can cause dehydration, overheating, and emotional lows after use.
Heroin Causes intense euphoria and pain relief; highly addictive and dangerous.
LSD (Acid) Creates hallucinations and mood swings; effects are unpredictable and can be frightening.
Methamphetamine (Meth) Increases energy and focus; long-term use leads to severe mental and physical health problems.
Prescription Opioids Relieve pain but can cause addiction, drowsiness, and overdose when misused.
